Setup Guide

Play Nintendo Switch Games on Android

Ryan Retro · Aug 22, 2025

In this guide I'm going to walk you through Nintendo Switch emulation on your Android device — whether you've got an Android handheld like the one I'm using here, or just an Android phone. I've tried to make it as simple and easy to follow as possible, so even if you don't have much experience with this, you should be able to follow along with me from start to finish.

We'll download everything you need, get the emulator set up, sort out your controls, install drivers for better performance, and finish with a troubleshooting section for when a game won't behave. One quick note up front: I can't tell you where to get firmware, keys, or game files, and I don't endorse piracy — please buy and obtain your games legally.

Step 1: Download Eden

First you need an emulator — that's the app that actually plays the games. There are a few choices out there, but I'd recommend Eden: it's constantly being updated (the latest build often lands the same week), which matters a lot for Switch emulation. I'll link it below.

On the Eden releases page on GitHub, scroll down past the Windows builds to the Android section. You'll see three options — Android Standard, Android Optimized, and Android Legacy. To keep things simple, grab Android Standard (it even says "most users should use this"). Tap it to download, then hit Install. Don't open it yet, though — we need a couple more files first. Hit Done.

Step 2: Firmware & prod.keys

Before Eden will run anything, you need two more files: the Nintendo Switch firmware and a prod.keys file. I can't tell you where to get these — but if you search those terms, you'll find them easily enough.

You'll see lots of versions available. I recommend version 19.0.1 for both, and the important part is that your firmware and your prod.keys are the same version number. Eden currently needs firmware 19.0.1 or below — newer versions exist but aren't working well yet, which is exactly why I point you to 19.0.1. Once you've got matching 19.0.1 firmware and prod.keys, you're ready to set up the emulator.

Step 3: First-Time Setup

Open Eden and it'll guide you through setup. Allow the permissions it asks for (including notifications) so everything works as it should, then hit Get Started.

  • Keys: tap Select Keys, navigate to your prod.keys file, and select it. You'll see "keys successfully installed."
  • Firmware: tap Select Firmware and choose your firmware ZIP. A little progress bar fills up and it confirms the firmware imported successfully.

Step 4: Add Your Games

Last piece — your games. (Again, I can't tell you where to get these, and please don't ask. Buy them legally.) Tap Add Games and navigate to where your games live. You're not picking a single game here — you select the whole folder so Eden can populate your full library, so hit Use This Folder and allow access.

You'll then get a Deep Scan option. Deep scan looks inside any sub-folders nested in the folder you picked, not just the top level. If your games are tucked inside folders-within-folders — or you're just not sure — I'd leave deep scan on so it catches everything. Hit OK, then Continue. It'll warn you this is pre-alpha software that may have bugs; tap "don't show again" and you're in.

Your games fill up a swipeable carousel by default. If you'd rather, tap the little eye icon at the top to switch to a list or a grid — grid is usually the most popular for quickly scanning a big library. Tap any game to launch it.

Step 5: Get Your Controls Working

Launch a game and you'll probably hit two things: a cluttered screen full of on-screen buttons, and — depending on your emulator and device — controls that don't respond. (On the newest Eden the buttons often just work, but let's set them up properly anyway.)

The key trick to remember: swipe in from the left edge of the screen to open the in-game settings. This works in Eden and the other Switch emulators too. From there:

  • Controls → Player 1 → Auto-Map a Controller. Your gamepad shows up (mine appears as an Xbox Wireless Controller); tap it and everything maps automatically. Back out and your controls will be working.
  • Hide the clutter: swipe in from the left again → Overlay Options. Toggle off the top two options to remove the pink text at the top (frame rate, device info), then toggle off Show Overlay to remove the on-screen gamepad. (If you're on a phone with no physical buttons, keep the overlay — you'll need it.)

Step 6: Install Turnip Drivers

You can already play a lot of games at this point — but if performance is rough or a game won't load, the first thing to do (especially on a Snapdragon device) is install a Turnip driver. These can massively improve performance and even get non-working games running.

The great news with the version of Eden I recommend: there's a driver downloader built right in. Tap the cog wheel at the top → GPU Driver Manager → Fetch, and you'll see drivers you can grab without leaving the app, including a recommended one for your device (mine is the Mr. Purple T19). Just pick a driver, show downloads, and tap the ZIP to download and apply it — no hunting through GitHub.

You can still install drivers manually if you want a specific one: download the auto.zip from a release's Assets, then cog wheel → GPU Driver Manager → Install → pick the ZIP. Not sure which driver suits your game and device? That's exactly what ryanretro.com is for — thousands of compatibility reports showing what works well for which game on which handheld, so you can just copy what already works.

Per-Game Drivers & Game Updates

There's no one-size-fits-all driver, so it helps to know the difference between your global and per-game settings:

  • Default driver (all games): the cog wheel at the top → GPU Driver Manager sets the driver for your whole library.
  • One game only: hold your finger on a game for a second to open that game's settings. Changing GPU Driver Manager here only affects that single game — everything else keeps the default.

That same per-game menu is where you install add-ons — updates, DLC, mods, or cheats. The most common is a game update: choose Update, point it at your update file, and watch it install. Once it's done you can toggle the update on or off with a checkbox (leave it checked to play the latest version). If you'd like to merge your games with their updates and DLC into one tidy file, I made a separate video showing exactly how.

Troubleshooting Common Problems

If you followed along, you should already have a solid Switch setup with plenty of games running. But here are the issues that come up most, and how to fix them:

  • A game doesn't show in the list. Check the file type — Switch games should be NSP or XCI (a game still inside a ZIP won't appear). If it's nested in folders, make sure you enabled deep scan. Seeing nothing at all? You probably picked the wrong folder — use the big folder button at the bottom of the game page to re-select it. If just one or two newer games are missing, you may need newer firmware (get 19.0.1).
  • A game won't open, or crashes after a few seconds. The fix that works most often is installing the game's update. Find an update file, apply it, and that usually sorts it. Trying a different driver can help too.
  • Performance is really bad. Try different drivers — there's genuinely no universal best. In Eden's Fetch section I've had great results with the new Mr. Purple T21, T20, and T19 across lots of devices. And check ryanretro.com to copy what's worked for others.
  • A game has bugs or glitches. Update the game, try a different driver, and know that some games need specific settings or mods (A Link to the Past, for example, needs a mod to stop the light flickering). Join a community like my Discord to see what others are doing.
  • Some games just won't run yet. The Snapdragon Elite is notoriously troublesome right now, and non-Snapdragon devices struggle because they don't get Turnip drivers. Sometimes it's a waiting game — Eden's changelog keeps unlocking titles that wouldn't launch before.

Wrapping Up

Do everything in this guide and you'll be in a really strong position to play Nintendo Switch games on your Android device. One last reminder: I don't endorse piracy in any way — please buy your games and obtain them legally.
